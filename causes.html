Understanding the Causes

Climate change is primarily driven by human activities that increase greenhouse gas emissions, altering the Earth's atmosphere and temperature regulation.

What's Causing Climate Change?

While natural factors play a role in climate variations, scientific evidence confirms that human activities are the dominant cause of the rapid warming observed since the mid-20th century.

97% of climate scientists agree that climate-warming trends are extremely likely due to human activities

Primary Causes of Climate Change

Here are the key human activities contributing to greenhouse gas emissions and climate change:

Burning Fossil Fuels

The combustion of coal, oil, and natural gas to generate electricity, power transportation, and heat buildings releases large amounts of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. This is the largest source of greenhouse gas emissions globally.

i

Switch to renewable energy providers or install solar panels to reduce your home's carbon footprint.

Deforestation

Forests act as carbon sinks, absorbing CO2 from the atmosphere. When forests are cleared for agriculture, urban development, or timber, this stored carbon is released back into the atmosphere, and the capacity to absorb carbon is reduced.

i

Support reforestation projects and use paper products made from sustainable or recycled sources.

Agriculture & Livestock

Agricultural practices, especially livestock production, release methane and nitrous oxide—potent greenhouse gases. Livestock digestive processes, manure management, and rice cultivation are significant sources of methane emissions.

i

Reducing meat consumption by even one day per week can significantly decrease your carbon footprint.

Industrial Processes

Many industrial processes, such as cement production, chemical manufacturing, and metal production, release greenhouse gases as byproducts of chemical reactions and energy use. These industries contribute significantly to global emissions.

i

Look for products made with low-carbon manufacturing processes and support companies with strong climate commitments.

Waste & Landfills

When organic waste decomposes in landfills without oxygen, it produces methane. Waste incineration also releases carbon dioxide. Improving waste management and reducing waste can help mitigate these emissions.

i

Composting food waste can reduce methane emissions from landfills and create nutrient-rich soil.

Fluorinated Gases

These synthetic gases, used in refrigeration, air conditioning, and industrial processes, have a much higher global warming potential than CO2—some thousands of times more potent. Though released in smaller quantities, their impact is significant.

i

When replacing appliances, choose models that use climate-friendly refrigerants with lower global warming potential.

The Greenhouse Effect

Understanding how greenhouse gases trap heat in the Earth's atmosphere is key to comprehending climate change:

How the Greenhouse Effect Works

The greenhouse effect is a natural process that warms the Earth's surface. When the sun's energy reaches the Earth's atmosphere, some of it is reflected back to space and the rest is absorbed and re-radiated by greenhouse gases.

Greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide, methane, and water vapor act like a blanket, trapping heat in the atmosphere and warming the planet. This natural greenhouse effect makes Earth habitable.

However, human activities are enhancing this natural greenhouse effect by adding more greenhouse gases to the atmosphere, causing the Earth to warm to unprecedented levels and at unprecedented rates.

Calculate Your Carbon Footprint

Understanding your personal contribution to climate change is the first step toward making meaningful changes:

Transportation

Daily commuting, air travel, and vehicle use contribute significantly to your carbon footprint. A single long-haul flight can emit as much carbon as some people produce in an entire year.

i

Walking, cycling, or using public transport for daily commuting can reduce your transportation emissions by up to 80%.

Home Energy Use

Heating, cooling, and powering your home requires energy—often derived from fossil fuels. Energy-efficient appliances and renewable energy sources can significantly reduce this impact.

i

Lowering your thermostat by just 1°C can reduce your heating bills by up to 10% while saving carbon emissions.

Food Consumption

Food production, especially meat and dairy, generates significant greenhouse gas emissions. Diet choices, food waste reduction, and local sourcing can all help reduce your food carbon footprint.

i

A plant-based diet can reduce your food carbon footprint by up to 73% compared to a diet high in animal products.
